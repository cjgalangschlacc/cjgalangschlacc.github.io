A Summary of Yesterday's Trillion Peso March

On September 21, 2025, 53rd anniversary of Martial Law to commemorate the, Filipinos from all walks of life came together in a historic statewide demonstration known as the Trillion Peso March. The demonstration was in response to reports of widespread corruption in flood control projects, which allegedly cost ₱1.9 trillion in lost revenue due to kickbacks, abandoned projects, and inadequate infrastructure. The date was picked in order to symbolically link corruption in the present with abuses of power in the past.

The Trillion Peso March was a multi-sectoral, interfaith, and decentralized protest that spanned across Metro Manila and key provincial cities:

Event Schedule

LOCATION TIME ORGANIZERS
Luneta Park |9:00 AM| Led by student and activist groups under the banner “Baha sa Luneta”
EDSA People Power Monument |2:00 PM| Organized by church leaders and civil society groups

Participants

  • Over 30,000 people in Metro Manila alone
  • Celebrities like Vice Ganda, Anne Curtis, Angel Locsin, and Kim Chiu joined the rallies
  • Church leaders including Cardinal Pablo Virgilio David, Bishop Bagaforo, and Bishop Gaa led prayers and speeches

Key Messages

  • “Ikulong na ‘yan! Mga korakot!” echoed across EDSA
  • Protesters wore white shirts and carried balloon crocodiles to symbolize corrupt officials
  • Calls for accountability extended to Vice President Sara Duterte over confidential fund controversies

Regional Mobilization

  • Cebu City: 700 protesters marched peacefully with 400 police deployed
  • Baguio, Iloilo, Bacolod, Davao: Local rallies echoed the national call for justice

A powerful example of public outrage, solidarity, and civic duty was the Trillion Peso March. Its demands for justice, change, and transparency went beyond political affiliations. The nonviolent but fervent protest demonstrated that Filipinos will no longer put up with structural corruption. The way institutions react will determine if this movement results in long-lasting change, but the people have made their voices known clearly: "Sobra na." Tama na.
